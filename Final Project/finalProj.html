Land Ice

The most significant contributor to the critical rise in sea level, and a significant piece of evidence of climate change is the melting land ice. Land ice includes glaciers and ice sheets that cover areas such as Antarctica and Greenland. The exposure of land ice to heat results in it melting and due to the rise in temperature over the years, land ice has been melting at an alarming rate. According to NASA, the ice mass of Antarctica has been dropping at a rate of 125.0 ± 39 Gigatonnes per year and the ice mass of Greenland has been dropping at a rate of 287.0 Gigatonnes per year with a margin of ±21 since 2002. One gigatonne is equivalent to 2204622621848.8 lbs, so imagine how much ice is being lost per year! Upon melting, the water from the ice travels rapidly to the sea, filling it up and causing a rise in the sea level. (To see the effects of the rise in sea level, click on the sea level tab.) The rise in sea level and the massive lost of land ice demonstrates the dire effects of climate change.

So how does the melting land ice affect us?

  • The increasing temperature in the Arctic leads to the thawing of the frozen ground of the Arctic, which is known as permafrost. This layer holds carbon dioxide and methane, stoking fear in scientists that if this layer were to continue melt, the chemicals would be released and further contribute to the warming of our planet.
  • Snow covered-areas reflect sunlight. When melting ice reveals bodies of water that is darker, heat is absorbed rather than reflected. The absorption of heat will lead to that area warming up significantly. Eventually, global temperatures would be affected too.
